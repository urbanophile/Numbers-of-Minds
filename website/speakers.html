Speakers

Rintaro Higuchi - International Center for Materials Nanoarchitectonics

My research interest is in the dynamics of the conductive network and brain-like computing based on the network materials. The networks composed of conductive polymer or polymer-coated silver nanowire exhibited spontaneous fluctuation, which behaviors are analogous with biological network system such as neuron activity. I’m presently studying on the investigation of network dynamics, particularly silver nanowire network, using scanning probe microscopy and fluorescent microscopy, and also trying to apply these materials to practical devices.

Kewei Chen - Banner Alzheimer’s Institute

Prof. Kewei Chen got his Ph.D. Degree in Biomathematics at School of Medicine, UCLA 1993. He is currently the Director, senior Scientist and senior Biomathematician, the Computational Image Analysis and Research Program, Banner Alzheimer’s Institute (BAI), Banner Health; the director of Data Management & Statistical Core, Arizona AD Core Center under National Alzheimer’s Coordinating Center. He also holds adjunct research professorships at Shanghai Jiao Tong University, Beijing Normal University, Arizona State University and University of Arizona.

Soroosh Warwick - Institute of Advanced Study (IAS), University of Warwick

Soroosh’s research focuses on functional connectivity of the human brain. Using neuroimaging techniques and statistical methods, he develops frameworks aiming to facilitate group inference to gain a better understanding into changes in the human brain due to neuropsychiatric disorders. He completed his PhD in 2016 at the Institute of Digital Healthcare, University of Warwick. His thesis was focused on developing network statistical methods to describe the human ‘connectomics’ in healthy and disordered subjects. Prior to his PhD, in 2012, he graduated in Electronic Engineering from the University of Birmingham. Since October 2016, he has undertaken a fellowship at the Institute of Advanced Study (IAS), University of Warwick. His research at IAS is focused on the novel area of ‘population connectomics’ which aims to integrate topological features of the brain with other available phenotypes through large-scale data-sets to explore the interaction between the human brain, demographics, behavior and lifestyle.

Genevera Allen - Rice University

My research interests are in developing statistical tools to help scientists understand massive amounts of so-called "Big-Data". Technological advances in medicine, engineering, the Internet, and finance have produced larger and more complex data sets. By using techniques from high-dimensional inference, machine learning, and convex optimization, I solve statistical problems arising from these new technologies. In particular, my methodological and theoretical work lies in the areas of modern multivariate analysis, graphical models, statistical machine learning, and the emerging area of data integration. My applied research interests include neuroimaging, neural recordings, and high-throughput genomics.

Elisabeth Sweeney - Rice University

Recently, Elizabeth’s focus is on Alzheimer’s disease (AD), arguably one of the largest public health crises of our time. As part of a prestigious postdoctoral fellowship program at Rice University, Elizabeth is diving into large high-dimensional neuroimaging, genetic and epidemiological data and discovering biomarkers of cognitive reserve, or the difference among individuals that enables some to be more resilient to the brain changes associated with AD than others.

Raquel Prado - University of California Santa Cruz

Raquel is Professor of Statistics in the Department of Applied Mathematics and Statistics at the University of California Santa Cruz. Her main areas of research interests include Bayesian time series modeling and statistical models and methods for brain data.

Thomas Carlson - The University of Sydney

A/Prof Carlson is an ARC Future Fellow in the School of Psychology at the University of Sydney. Prior to coming to the University of Sydney, he was a research fellow at Harvard and the University of Utrecht (the Netherlands), an Assistant Professor at the University of Maryland (UMD), and a senior lecturer at Macquarie University.

Joana Grah - University of Cambridge

Joana studied mathematics at the University of Muenster in Germany and received her Bachelor’s and Master’s degree in 2012 and 2014, respectively. After having been working in the Imaging workgroup of Prof. Martin Burger in Muenster, she started her PhD studies at the University of Cambridge in October 2014. Her supervisors are Dr Carola-Bibiane Schoenlieb, head of the Cambridge Image Analysis group at the Department of Applied Mathematics and Theoretical Physics, and Dr Stefanie Reichelt, head of the Light Microscopy Core Facility at the Cancer Research UK Cambridge Institute.

Veronica Corona - University of Cambridge

Veronica studied Bioengineering at University of Cagliari in Italy and TU Delft in The Netherlands where she received her Bachelor's and Master's degree, respectively. She spent the final year of her MSc at the University of Cambridge where, supervised by Dr. Carola-Bibiane Schoenlieb and Prof. Jan Lellmann, she worked on her master's project on image segmentation and analysis on neurological structures. Veronica started her PhD studies at the University of Cambridge in October 2015. Her supervisors are Dr Carola-Bibiane Schoenlieb, head of the Cambridge Image Analysis group at the Department of Applied Mathematics and Theoretical Physics, Dr Stefanie Reichelt, head of the Light Microscopy Core Facility at the Cancer Research UK Cambridge Institute, and Prof. Kevin Brindle, head of the Molecular Imaging at the Cancer Research UK Cambridge Institute.

Maxwell Bennett - The University of Sydney

Maxwell Bennett is Professor of Neuroscience and holds the first University Chair for ‘research recognized internationally to be of exceptional distinction’ He graduated in Electrical Engineering and did his doctoral research in Zoology at Melbourne University. He then turned to the brain sciences and was appointed to a Personal Chair at Sydney University, after the first Personal Chair Lord May, at which time he was awarded the largest personal Centre of Research Excellence by the Australian Government. His over 300 papers are concerned with research on synaptic connections between nerve cells in the brain.

Peter Robinson - The University of Sydney

Peter Robinson received his PhD in theoretical physics from the University of Sydney in 1987, then held a postdoc at the University of Colorado at Boulder until 1990. He then returned to Australia, joining the permanent staff of the School of Physics at the University of Sydney in 1993, obtaining a chair in 2000, and subsequently holding two Australian Research Council Federation Fellowships before his present Laureate Fellowship. His current work is mainly on brain dynamics, networks, complex systems, field theory, propagators, critical phenomena, imaging, and waves. He is a CI on the ARC CoE for Integrative Brain Function, the CRC for Alertness, Safety, and Productivity, the NHMRC CRE Neurosleep, and the University of Sydney Center for Complex Systems.
